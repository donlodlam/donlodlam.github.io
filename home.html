Knowledge Organization project

This is a project realized by Davide Apolloni and Hizkiel Mitiku, students of Digital Humanities and Digital Knowledge at Bologna’s University. The project was realized after having attended the course of Knowledge Organization and Digital Methods in the Cultural Heritage Domain, held by Professor Francesca Tomasi in the academic year 2018/2019.

Knowledge Organization is a branch of library and information science concerned with activities such as document description, indexing, and classification performed in libraries, databases, archives, etc. The specific use of this discipline inside this project focuses mostly on the ontologies and the vocabularies that are used to describe data and enrich them through semantic strategies. The final result involves the creation of complex metadata for digital objects related to the cultural heritage domain.

By browsing the website of CulturaItalia we found many interesting results related to Don Quixote. We decided to choose Giuseppe Bonito’s painting because it depicts one of the most famous episodes of Cervantes’ masterpiece, i.e. Don Quixote fighting against the windmills which looked like giants to his insane eyes.

 

This item is used to describe the person (character) rather than the concept. In fact, the first concept which this image could entail is concerned with the mental insanity which led Don Quixote to make this absurd action. As a matter of fact, when launching himself towards these “giants”, the knight of the sorrowful figure is not thinking about fighting for the justice, but he simply wants to act as one of the heroes who populated the stories he has been reading for his entire life.

Another important item which came to our mind when thinking about Don Quixote is the famous song written and sung by the Italian songwriter Francesco Guccini. The lyrics of this song is extremely rich and detailed, therefore this music could be used to express features related to both the main character of our project, i.e. Don Quixote, and the concept around which our idea develops, i.e. the journey for the justice which leads Don Quixote and his loyal and pragmatic companion Sancho Panza through incredible (dis)adventures.
Coming back to the song, we found useful and interesting metadata by examining the website “MusicBrainz”.


https://musicbrainz.org/recording/dbd11e86-b6aa-4ad7-8978-861911e50e6a

MusicBrainz is an open music encyclopedia which collects music metadata and makes them available to the public.
Like Wikipedia, MusicBrainz is maintained by a global community of users and everyone can participate and contribute.


By typing “Don Chisciotte” in the search bar, we found the information we were looking for.


There are two official recordings. The first one is taken from the album “Stagioni”, while the second one is taken from a CD which collects Guccini’s most famous songs, which is why some information which is marked with question marks cannot be derived.
By clicking on “Stagioni”, one is redirected to a list which includes all the songs that are part of the Album. The number “7” (i.e. 1.7) points out that “Don Chisciotte” is the seventh song of the album.



The fingerprint includes two links to AcoustID, a big database for audio fingerprints. Here we can find more data which are referred to the MusicBrainz metadata identifiers.



By clicking on Aliases (alternate name for an entity. They typically contain common misspellings or variations of the name and are also used to improve search results) and Tags we could not find any result.

Instead, interesting and useful information can be derived by the Details section.

Interestingly enough, we noticed that the MBID is included in the second line of the XML tree: Right after, we got more information concerning the title and the length of the song and more information about the songwriter who wrote this piece of music.


The original title given by Guccini is followed by the MBID (MusicBrainz Identifier), a 36 character Universally Unique Identifier that is permanently assigned to each entity in the database.

Successively, one can obtain the permalink, i.e. a URL which is intended to remain unchanged for many years into the future.

Metadata Standard

The metadata standard which are used here are actually two: MARCXML and MODS. MARCXML is the XML representation of MARC 21 data, the standard used so as to represent and communicate descriptive metadata about library items.

Instead, MODS (Metadata Object Description Standard) is the XML markup for metadata from existing MARC 21 records and original resource description.

A complete explanation of all the standards that are used by the Library of Congress can be viewed here: https://www.loc.gov/librarians/standards

Metadata standard

The metadata standard used by Europeana is EDM (Europeana Data Model).

The first data model that was used by Europeana was ESE, but it still had gaps. These gaps are due to the fact that library, museums and archives, when digitizing their cultural heritage objects, all use different metadata standards.

EDM can go beyond the domain-specific metadata standards preserving the richness of community standards such as LIDO for museums, EAD for archives or METS of digital libraries.

Hence, EDM can be seen as a semantic web-based framework which facilitates Europeana’s participation in the Semantic Web.

EDM was developed thanks to the cooperation of technical experts from libraries, museums, archives and audio-visual collection domains. Moreover, it can arrange in a good way standards such as DC, EAD and LIDO.

EDM contributes to the creation of new knowledge, by collecting, connecting and even enriching metadata. Thanks to this metadata standard, Europeana can represent data associated to one and the same object, and can also show multiple views of the object, being it digital or physical.

Despite the metadata provided are many and detailed, there is no information concerning the metadata standard which is used by the British Museum.


Therefore, we analyzed the metadata standards which are available and tried to understand which is the one implemented by the British Museum.


After the analysis, we deduced that the metadata standard here used is CONA, the Cultural Objects Name Authority. It is a structured vocabulary containing authority records for cultural works, including architecture and movable works such as paintings, sculpture, prints, etc., whether extant, destroyed, or conceptual.

Metadata Standard

The Biblioteca Nacional de España uses six metadata standards, according to the item which is describing.

The metadata standards used by this library are Dublin Core, PREMIS, METS, MODS and MARCXML.The full documentation concerning the metadata used in this library can be read here: http://www.bne.es/gl/Inicio/Perfiles/Bibliotecarios/NormasInternacionales/Metadatos/index.html

La Florida del Inca

La Florida del Inca

[BOOK] La Florida del Inca


http://tinyurl.com/y29r5qxc


An item related to the date of publication of El Quijote, namely, 1605 is represented by the book La florida del Inca, historical book written by Garcilaso de la Vega which was published the same year as Cervantes’ masterpiece. Strangely enough, both de la Vega and Cervantes died the same year.

The connection between the two Spanish writers is even stronger, as are both considered two of the main innovators of Spanish culture and style of writing.

We found this item in the OPAC SBN (Servizio Bibliotecario Nazionale), a network of Italian libraries coordinated by ICCU (Istituto centrale per il catalogo unico delle biblioteche italiane e per le informazioni bibliografiche) whose aim concerns the necessary collaboration of all the Italian libraries in order to create a broad and complete catalog.

Metadata Standard

The metadata standard used here is the ISBD, International Standard Bibliographic Description. This standard is aimed at making universally and promptly available, in a form that is internationally acceptable, basic bibliographic data for all published resources in all countries.

The metadata standards are therefore organized according to the ISBD schema, whose general rules can be summarized as it follows:

Number DEFINITION
1 Title and statement of responsibility area
2 Edition area
3 Material (or type of publication) specific area
4 Publication, distribution, etc., area
5 Physical description area
6 Series area
7 Note area
8 Standard number (or alternative) and terms of availability area

Another possible display for the metadata can be analyzed, and it follows the MARC21 schema, a standard used to represent and communicate descriptive metadata about library items.



The complete schema can be seen here: http://tinyurl.com/y2opqdnx

Metadata standard
